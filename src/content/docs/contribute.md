This guide welcomes contributions of any size and contributors of any skill level.
We are happy to help with guidance on documentation, git, and pull requests.

## About this guide

This guide is an Astro [Starlight](https://starlight.astro.build) site in a [git repository](https://github.com/anaxite/technical-writers-guide-to-ai).
Contributing means a few things: editing Markdown, opening a pull request, participating in discussions, and more.

There is no formal editorial process yet, but we're working on it!

## Different kinds of contributions

There are many ways to contribute to this guide:

1. Submit a GitHub issue to propose a change, talk about an error, or ask a question.
2. Start a GitHub discussion to get clarification or go deeper into a topic.
3. Edit a single page in GitHub, and submit the edit as a pull request.
4. Fork the repository to your own GitHub account, make multiple changes, and submit those changes as a pull request.

The repository is [anaxite/technical-writers-guide-to-ai](https://github.com/anaxite/technical-writers-guide-to-ai).

## The guide's principles

### General principles

This guide is intended to be *descriptive, on principled grounding* overall.
Durable, model-agnostic
principles are preferred ("don't let the AI do more than you understand and can validate"; "instead
of having the AI do the work, have it interview you about the work"; "trust a little,
verify a lot") as the grounding layer readers launch from.
This is the spine.

Behind that, the body of the guide documents how the practice *is*, not how it *ought to be*.

This spine is a starting position, not a commitment. It may change with experimentation.

### Subject matter categories

In general, this guide suggests there are three places AI meets documentation:

- **AI supporting content creation and management.** Tools that help you write, edit, organise, and maintain docs.
- **AI generating and publishing content.** Documentation produced with little human intervention.
- **AI reading your content.** Your pages as input to somebody else's system. Retrieval, assistants, and agents consume documentation written for humans.

The division is a convenience, not a taxonomy.

### Questions and contradictions

A page or a section may be a question with its corresponding answers. This structure has two consequences: 

1. **A question with no answers is a complete page.** It is a question the profession has not settled, published as such. That is information. It is also the most direct invitation the guide can make.
2. **A question with three contradictory answers is also a complete page.** If three writers took three incompatible approaches and each can say why, the page is honest.

The guide is not required to pick and mandate a certain approach, but it should be mindful that people may be after an approach.

### Durability and perishability

Every page separates two kinds of content, because they rot at different speeds.

The *durable* layer relates to how AI systems behave, the failure modes they produce, the questions worth asking, and the principles that survive a model release.
It should still be worth reading in a year.

The *perishable* layer contains tool names, model names, prices, screenshots, prompts, and benchmarks.
It is dated on sight, and it is meant to be cut without ceremony the moment it stops being true.
Be careful when adding this content.

If you are reading a page long after it was written, treat the durable half as current and the perishable half as history. Where the two are tangled together, that may be a defect in the page, and [it is worth fixing](/contribute/).

### What is not worth adding

Prompt collections without the context that made them work.
Vendor material.
Predictions.
Anything that would need rewriting the moment a model ships, and that the durable/perishable split cannot rescue.
